**1. About the Author and the Text**
**Khushwant Singh (1915–2014)** was one of India's best-known writers and journalists. This chapter is an **autobiographical sketch** — the author is describing his own grandmother from memory. The title compares his written description to a painted "portrait": instead of using a brush, he paints her picture with words.

**2. Genre:** Autobiographical prose / character sketch

**3. Central theme:** The loving bond between a grandmother and grandson, how it changes with time and modern education, and the quiet dignity with which the grandmother faces life and death.

**4. Difficult words:**- ***mantelpiece*** — shelf above a fireplace- ***fables*** — moral stories (often with animals); here, the children treated her childhood stories as unbelievable tales- ***hobbled*** — walked with difficulty- ***stoop*** — bent posture of the body- ***rosary*** — string of prayer beads- ***puckered*** — wrinkled- ***serenity***— calmness, peace

***monotonous sing-song*** — a dull, repetitive musical tone (her style of praying)- ***scriptures*** — holy religious books- ***verandah*** — open, roofed porch of a house

***lewd associations*** — connections with vulgar/indecent things- ***monopoly*** — exclusive possession of something- ***harlots*** — (old word) immoral women- ***gentlefolk*** — respectable people of good family

***Sagging*** — generally means drooping, sinking, or settling downwards under weight, pressure, or a lack of tautness-***dilapidated*** — old, falling to pieces- ***pallor*** — unhealthy pale colour- ***shroud*** — cloth used to cover a dead body- ***customary*** — according to tradition

**5. Important Expressions from the Text (with Annotations)**

These are the seven expressions the textbook asks you to infer from context. Learn them well — they are frequently asked in exams.

**Expression:** **"the thought was almost revolting"** 

**Simple meaning**:The idea was shocking/unpleasant to imagine 

**Annotation (Why it is used)**: The author could not imagine his old, wrinkled grandmother as ever having been young and pretty. To him, she had ***always*** been old. 

**Expression: "an expanse of pure white serenity"\*\*** 

**Simple meaning**: A wide stretch of calm whiteness 

 **Annotation (Why it is used)**: She is compared to a snow-covered winter mountain landscape — her white hair, white clothes, and peaceful nature made her look serene and beautiful. 

**Expression**: **"a turning-point"** 

**Simple meaning**: A moment when things change completely 

**Annotation (Why it is used)**: Moving to the city changed their friendship forever — she could no longer go to school with him.  

**Expression: "accepted her seclusion with resignation"**

**Simple meaning**: Quietly accepted being left alone, without complaining 

**Annotation (Why it is used)**: When the author got his own room at university, she did not protest. She calmly accepted loneliness as part of life. 

**Expression**:  **"a veritable bedlam of chirrupings"** 

**Simple meaning**: A real scene of noisy chaos made by chirping birds 

**Annotation (Why it is used)**:  Hundreds of sparrows gathered around her, chirping loudly, when she fed them. ("Bedlam" = a place of noisy confusion.) 

**Expression**:**"frivolous rebukes"**

**Simple meaning**: Light-hearted, playful scoldings (not serious) 

**Annotation (Why it is used)**: She scolded the sparrows lovingly, the way one teases someone one loves. 

**Expression**:**"the sagging skins of the dilapidated drum"**

**Simple meaning**:The loose, worn-out surface of an old, broken-down drum 

**Annotation (Why it is used)**: On the author's homecoming, she celebrated by beating an old drum and singing — unusual joy for a woman who otherwise only prayed.
